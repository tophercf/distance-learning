Could Online Courses Help Close the Gap?

With the rise of online courses, many educational institutions have expanded or started a distance learning option.

In the graph to the right, we see with the aggregated data collected by HarvardX and MITX that the Massive Online Learning format has grown in popularity year over year.

Using the dropdown, check out the trends for these two universities leveraging remote learning.

The four functional areas we looked at include: - CS: Computer Science - GHSS: Government, Health, and Social Science - HHRDE: Humanities, History, Design, Religion, and Education - STEM: Science, Technology, Engineering, and Mathematics

It's not just Harvard and MIT Either...

Over the span of 2004-2017, the number of institutions that added remote learning opportunities increased by 30%. This is an unprecedented shift into alternative learning and based on the trend, it's here to stay.

Who is sticking with online learning?


Despite an overall increase in online learners, not all strata of society are seeing proportional growth in participation. Trends differ signficantly between graduate and undergraduate students, with low- and high-earning graduate students seeing dramatic shifts, with online learners from wealthier backgrounds gaining relative to other areas. Conversely, undergraduates over the same period were relatively stable, and saw high participation from lower income groups. Explore the data with the below button toggle:



While online learning has been framed as a tool for democratizing education for all incomes and abilities, the data here indicate that groups joining up and sticking with online learning are relatively fractured. For upper-level courses and specializations, top earners are increasingly represented. This calls into question all over again: who really benefits from online learning? And is it truly as egalitarian as it's made out to be?
